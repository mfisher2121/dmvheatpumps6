# When Heat Pumps Struggle: Temperature Limits & Backup Heat Explained

The #1 objection to heat pumps:
"Yeah, but what happens when it gets really cold?"
Usually followed by: "My neighbor got one and they froze all winter" or "I heard they don't work below freezing."
Here's what's actually true:
Heat pumps absolutely lose efficiency in extreme cold. That's physics, not a flaw.
But "extreme cold" means different things to different systems, and Maryland winters are manageable for modern equipment.

## Understanding Heat Pump Performance in Cold Weather

First, let's be clear about what happens as temperature drops:
Heat pumps don't suddenly "stop working" at a magic temperature. They gradually become less efficient.
Think of it like this:
* At 47°F outside: Heat pump is a superhero (COP of 3.5-4.0 = 350-400% efficient)
* At 32°F outside: Heat pump is still really good (COP of 3.0-3.5 = 300-350% efficient)
* At 17°F outside: Heat pump is working but not amazing (COP of 2.5-3.0 = 250-300% efficient)
* At 5°F outside: Heat pump is struggling but still beating resistance heat (COP of 2.0-2.5 = 200-250% efficient)
* Below 5°F: Heat pump might need help, depending on model

Why efficiency drops:
1. Less heat available to extract (colder air has less thermal energy)
2. Bigger temperature gap (harder to move heat from 15°F outside to 68°F inside than from 45°F to 68°F)
3. More defrost cycles (system has to periodically melt ice off outdoor coil)
4. Refrigerant physics (refrigerant becomes less effective at extreme temperatures)

But here's the critical point:
Even at its worst, a heat pump is still 2x more efficient than electric resistance heat.
So "struggling" doesn't mean "not working." It means "not as incredibly efficient as usual."

## The Technology Gap: Old vs. New Heat Pumps

This is where most confusion comes from.
Old heat pumps (pre-2010):
* Effective down to: 35-40°F
* Started losing serious capacity: 30°F
* Backup heat kicked in: 25°F
* Became useless below: 15-20°F
These are the systems your neighbor complains about.

Modern standard heat pumps (2015-2020):
* Effective down to: 25-30°F
* Start losing significant capacity: 20°F
* Backup heat kicks in: 15-20°F
* Still operate (reduced capacity): Down to 5-10°F
Better, but still not ideal for Maryland's coldest days.

Cold-climate heat pumps (2018-2024):
* Effective down to: 5°F to -5°F
* Still heating at reduced capacity: Down to -15°F to -25°F (depending on model)
* Backup heat rarely needed in Maryland
These are what you should be installing in 2024.

## Maryland Winter Reality Check

Let's look at actual Frederick, MD weather data (typical for Maryland):
Average winter low temperatures:
* December: 25-30°F
* January: 20-25°F
* February: 22-27°F
* Cold snaps (5-15°F): Happen ~5-10 days per winter
* Extreme cold (below 5°F): Maybe 1-3 days per winter, some years not at all

**What this means:**
95% of winter, your cold-climate heat pump is operating at 80-100% efficiency with no backup needed.
5% of winter, it might need a little help on the coldest 2-3 days of the year.

## What Actually Happens on the Coldest Days

### Scenario: It's 10°F outside at 6 AM

**With a cold-climate heat pump:**
1. Heat pump keeps running (doesn't shut off)
2. Capacity drops to 70-85% of rated output
3. System runs more continuously (longer cycles to maintain temperature)
4. Electricity usage increases (lower COP means more power needed)
5. House stays comfortable (assuming it's well-insulated and properly sized)
6. Backup heat may kick in briefly to bridge gap
7. Energy use: 67 kWh for 24 hours at 67 kWh × $0.14 = **$9.38 for the day**

**If your house is poorly insulated or system is undersized:**
1. Heat pump runs continuously but can't quite keep up
2. Indoor temperature might drop 1-3°F below setpoint
3. Backup heat may kick in to bridge the gap
4. Electricity bill for that day is higher than usual
5. As soon as temperature rises above 15-20°F, heat pump catches up again
6. This is not a failure. This is expected behavior on the coldest 2-3 days of the year.

### When You Actually Need to Worry

**Normal cold-weather behavior:**
✅ Heat pump runs continuously on cold days  
✅ Defrost cycles every 60-90 minutes on humid days  
✅ Slight temperature drop (1-2°F) on coldest days  
✅ Backup heat occasionally kicks in below 20°F  
✅ Outdoor unit covered in frost between defrost cycles  

**Problems that need attention:**
❌ Heat pump shuts off entirely in cold weather  
❌ Defrost cycles every 15-20 minutes (something's wrong with sensor)  
❌ Never defrosts (outdoor coil becomes solid block of ice)  
❌ Indoor temp drops 5-7°F below setpoint on moderately cold day  
❌ Backup heat runs constantly even on mild days  
❌ Outdoor unit is constantly iced over  

**If you see problem signs, call your contractor immediately.**

## The Sizing Question

Contractors often oversize heat pumps "for cold days."
This is a mistake.

Here's why:
Oversized heat pump:
* Works great on coldest days (has excess capacity)
* Short-cycles in mild weather (constantly turning on/off)
* Never runs long enough to dehumidify in summer
* Less efficient overall
* Costs more upfront
* Payback period is longer than properly sized system

Properly sized heat pump:
* Runs longer cycles in mild weather (more efficient)
* Properly dehumidifies in summer
* Might need a little backup heat on coldest 2-3 days
* Costs less upfront
* Higher efficiency overall
* Better comfort

The right approach:
Size heat pump for typical winter conditions (25-35°F), not extreme conditions (5°F).
Use backup heat for the handful of days it gets colder than that.

## The Defrost Cycle: What's Happening When Your Heat Pump Looks Broken

You'll see this on cold, humid mornings:
Heat pump is running. Steam is pouring off the outdoor unit. Your vents are blowing slightly cool air for a few minutes.

**Don't panic. This is the defrost cycle.**

Why it happens:
When heat pump extracts heat from cold, humid air, moisture condenses on the outdoor coil and freezes into ice.
If ice builds up, it blocks airflow and kills efficiency.
So the system automatically reverses into cooling mode for 5-10 minutes to melt the ice.

**What you experience:**
* Outdoor unit steams (ice melting)
* Indoor vents blow room-temperature or slightly cool air temporarily
* Backup heat may kick in briefly to keep you comfortable during defrost
* This is normal. This is expected behavior, not a malfunction.

**How often it happens:**
* Warm days (above 35°F): Rarely
* Cold, humid days (25-35°F): Every 60-90 minutes
* Very cold, dry days (below 20°F): Less frequent (less moisture to freeze)

**If defrost cycles are happening every 15-20 minutes or lasting more than 10 minutes, call your contractor. Something's wrong.**

## How to Set Up Your Backup Heat Strategy

Most heat pump thermostats have these settings:

### 1. Backup Heat Lockout Temperature
**What it does:** Prevents backup heat from turning on unless outdoor temp drops below this threshold.
**Recommended setting for Maryland:**
* Standard heat pump: 30°F
* Cold-climate heat pump: 15-20°F

**Why:** Let heat pump do its job as long as it can. Backup heat is expensive.

### 2. Auxiliary Heat Temperature Offset
**What it does:** If indoor temp drops more than X degrees below setpoint, turn on backup heat.
**Recommended setting:** 2-3°F offset

### 3. Compressor Minimum Runtime
**What it does:** Prevents heat pump from short-cycling in cold weather.
**Recommended setting:** 10-15 minutes minimum

**Why:** Heat pumps need longer runtimes in cold weather to be efficient. Short cycling wastes energy.

### 4. Emergency Heat (Manual Override)
**What it does:** Forces system to use only backup heat, shuts off heat pump.
**When to use it:**
* Heat pump has failed and you're waiting for repair
* Outdoor coil is completely iced over and won't defrost
* Never use this routinely

**Common mistake:** People switch to "emergency heat" thinking it will warm house faster. It won't. It will just cost you way more money.
